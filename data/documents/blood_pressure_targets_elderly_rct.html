Abstract

Background

Current guidelines recommend intensive blood pressure control (<130/80 mmHg) for most adults, but evidence in adults ‚â•75 years remains limited. The original SPRINT trial included patients ‚â•75 years but was not powered for age-specific analyses.

Methods

We randomly assigned 8,456 adults ‚â•75 years with hypertension and high cardiovascular risk to intensive treatment (systolic BP target <120 mmHg) or standard treatment (systolic BP target <140 mmHg). The primary endpoint was a composite of cardiovascular death, nonfatal myocardial infarction, nonfatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure.

Results

CONTRADICTORY FINDING: Intensive blood pressure control in adults ‚â•75 years was associated with increased serious adverse events (23.7% vs 18.9%, p<0.001) without significant cardiovascular benefit (HR 0.89, 95% CI: 0.76-1.04, p=0.14). Falls requiring hospitalization were significantly increased (8.2% vs 5.1%, p<0.001).

Conclusions

In adults ‚â•75 years, intensive blood pressure control (<120 mmHg) does not provide cardiovascular benefit and increases serious adverse events compared to standard control (<140 mmHg). These findings challenge current age-agnostic blood pressure guidelines.

Introduction

Hypertension affects over 80% of adults aged 75 years and older, making it the most common cardiovascular risk factor in this population. Current American Heart Association/American College of Cardiology (AHA/ACC) guidelines recommend a blood pressure target of <130/80 mmHg for most adults, regardless of age.

However, older adults have unique physiological characteristics that may alter the risk-benefit profile of intensive blood pressure control, including:

  • Increased arterial stiffness and isolated systolic hypertension
  • Higher prevalence of orthostatic hypotension
  • Increased fall risk and frailty
  • Multiple comorbidities and polypharmacy
  • Reduced life expectancy affecting long-term benefit realization

Study Hypothesis

Primary Hypothesis: Intensive blood pressure control (systolic BP <120 mmHg) will reduce cardiovascular events compared to standard control (systolic BP <140 mmHg) in adults ‚â•75 years.

Safety Hypothesis: Intensive control will not significantly increase serious adverse events in this elderly population.

Methods

Study Design

SPRINT-Senior was a multicenter, randomized, open-label, controlled trial conducted at 156 clinical sites across 12 countries. The study was designed as a definitive trial to assess intensive vs standard blood pressure control specifically in adults ‚â•75 years.

Participants

Inclusion Criteria

  • Age ‚â•75 years
  • Systolic blood pressure 130-180 mmHg
  • High cardiovascular risk (‚â•15% 10-year ASCVD risk or existing cardiovascular disease)
  • Able to provide informed consent

Exclusion Criteria

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • History of stroke
  • Chronic kidney disease (eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m¬≤)
  • Heart failure with ejection fraction <35%
  • Life expectancy <3 years
  • Systolic BP <130 mmHg on current therapy

Randomization and Interventions

Participants were randomly assigned 1:1 to:

  • Intensive Treatment Group: Systolic BP target <120 mmHg
  • Standard Treatment Group: Systolic BP target <140 mmHg

Blood pressure medications were adjusted according to a standardized protocol using ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers, and thiazide diuretics as first-line agents.

Outcomes

Primary Endpoint

Composite of cardiovascular death, nonfatal myocardial infarction, nonfatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure

Secondary Endpoints

  • All-cause mortality
  • Cardiovascular mortality
  • Individual components of primary endpoint
  • Cognitive decline (assessed by Montreal Cognitive Assessment)
  • Quality of life (SF-36)

Safety Endpoints

  • Serious adverse events
  • Falls requiring medical attention
  • Syncope
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Electrolyte abnormalities
  • Hypotension-related hospitalizations

Discussion

Key Findings

  1. CONTRADICTS CURRENT GUIDELINES: Intensive blood pressure control (<120 mmHg) in adults ‚â•75 years did not provide significant cardiovascular benefit compared to standard control (<140 mmHg)
  2. INCREASED HARM: Intensive control significantly increased serious adverse events by 25%, including falls, syncope, and acute kidney injury
  3. AGE-DEPENDENT EFFECTS: Harm increased with advancing age, with strongly negative net clinical benefit in patients ‚â•85 years
  4. QUALITY OF LIFE IMPACT: Intensive control was associated with worse physical and mental quality of life scores

Clinical Implications

üö® Guideline Contradiction

These findings directly contradict current AHA/ACC guidelines that recommend blood pressure targets <130/80 mmHg for all adults regardless of age. Our results suggest that a "one-size-fits-all" approach may be inappropriate for older adults.

Age-Specific Considerations

The lack of cardiovascular benefit and increased adverse events in adults ‚â•75 years suggests that age should be a primary consideration in blood pressure target selection. The risk-benefit profile changes significantly with advancing age.

Individualized Approach

Rather than intensive targets for all elderly patients, a more individualized approach considering frailty, comorbidities, life expectancy, and patient preferences may be more appropriate.

Study Limitations

  • Open-label design may have introduced bias
  • Median follow-up of 3.8 years may not capture long-term benefits
  • Exclusion of patients with diabetes and stroke may limit generalizability
  • Predominantly white population (78%) may not represent all ethnic groups
  • Frailty assessment was limited to basic criteria

Conclusions

Primary Conclusion

CONTRADICTS CURRENT GUIDELINES: Intensive blood pressure control (systolic BP <120 mmHg) in adults ‚â•75 years does not provide cardiovascular benefit and significantly increases serious adverse events compared to standard control (systolic BP <140 mmHg).

Secondary Conclusions

  • Current age-agnostic blood pressure guidelines may be inappropriate for older adults
  • Age-specific blood pressure targets should be considered, with more conservative goals for patients ‚â•75 years
  • The risk-benefit profile of intensive blood pressure control becomes increasingly unfavorable with advancing age
  • Quality of life and functional status should be prioritized over intensive blood pressure control in elderly patients

Clinical Impact

These findings challenge fundamental assumptions about blood pressure management in older adults and suggest that millions of elderly patients worldwide may be receiving overly intensive treatment that provides no benefit while increasing harm. A paradigm shift toward age-specific, individualized blood pressure targets is warranted.
